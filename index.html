The Problem

Citizens have zero structured voice in discussing them.

India's constitutional amendment process is highly centralized, relying exclusively on elected representatives. Citizens have no structured channel to engage in constitutional discourse. Expert legal knowledge and collective public input remain underutilized. Existing platforms like MyGov.in address policy feedback, not constitutional participation. We built the missing infrastructure.

"WE THE PEOPLE OF INDIA" — transforming preamble text into participatory reality.
Educational Project Notice: This platform is an academic project developed by students of NIE Mysuru. It is not affiliated with or endorsed by the Government of India or any constitutional authority. To maintain platform integrity without active moderation, new post creation is currently disabled for public visitors. Users may explore the Constitution, read existing discussions, agree/disagree on proposals, and use the AI assistant freely.

Platform Features

Built for accountability,
designed for participation.

📖
Constitution Repository
Every article accessible as structured, readable data. Browse all 448 articles and linked citizen discussions.

Immutable Discussions
Modeled after GitHub Issues. No edits, no deletes after posting. Permanent accountability for every statement made.

Agree / Disagree Voting
Citizens vote on amendment suggestions. Each user votes once per post. Real-time counts reflect public sentiment.

Expert Validation Layer
Verified legal professionals review suggestions and add authoritative commentary, clearly distinguished from citizen posts.
🤖
NiyamAI — Constitutional Assistant
Powered by Google Gemini. Answers queries on articles, amendments, and landmark judgments with full constitutional context.

Trending Feed
Time-weighted algorithm surfaces the most actively discussed articles within a 48-hour window. Democracy in real time.

Civic Polls
Structured polling on constitutional questions — UCC, electoral reform, judicial appointments — with live results.

Role-Based Access
Citizen, Expert, and Lawmaker roles with differentiated permissions. Admin panel for moderation and platform integrity.

Legal Standing

We are a medium for citizens' voices.
Not a legislature. Not a petition. A suggestion platform.

Our platform does not amend the Constitution. It does not file petitions, does not have any legal effect on any constitutional provision, and does not represent any political position. It is a structured forum where citizens express informed opinions on constitutional matters. That activity is not only legal in India — it is constitutionally protected, constitutionally encouraged, and constitutionally necessary. The difference between our platform and Parliament is the same as the difference between a suggestion box and a legislature.

Constitutional Articles That Support Us

Article 19(1)(a)
Freedom of Speech and Expression
Every citizen has the fundamental right to express opinions on matters of public importance, including the Constitution. Our platform is a structured medium for that expression, no different from a newspaper that publishes op-eds on constitutional reform.
Article 19(1)(b)
Freedom to Assemble Peacefully
Our platform is a digital assembly — citizens gathering to exchange views on constitutional questions. The Supreme Court has held that digital spaces are extensions of traditional public forums for free expression.
Article 19(1)(c)
Freedom to Form Associations
Our platform creates a voluntary community of citizens interested in constitutional literacy and reform discourse. Participating in such a community is a fully protected associational activity.
Article 51A(b)(h)(j)
Fundamental Duties of Citizens
Article 51A(h) makes it a citizen's duty to develop the scientific temper and the spirit of inquiry and reform. Our platform directly operationalizes this duty. We are helping citizens fulfill their constitutional obligations, not just exercise rights.
Article 38
State to Secure Social Order for Welfare
Article 38 directs the State to minimize inequalities and ensure justice informs all national institutions. Our platform gives marginalized voices equal access to constitutional discourse, something traditional amendment processes do not provide.
Article 368
The Amendment Process Itself
Article 368 defines exactly how amendments happen: through Parliament, through a special majority. Our platform does none of this. The gap between citizen opinion and constitutional amendment is enormous and legally absolute. We operate entirely in the opinion space.

Laws and Frameworks That Explicitly Support Us

IT Act 2000
Information Technology Act 2000 and IT Rules 2021
Our platform is an intermediary under the IT Act. We do not generate harmful content and we provide a grievance redressal mechanism through our admin and reporting system. The IT Rules 2021 require content moderation — we have built exactly that with our admin panel, reporting system, and immutable post policy.
RTI 2005
Right to Information Act 2005
RTI established that citizens have a right to participate in governance through information access. Our platform is the next logical step: not just accessing information about the Constitution but engaging with it. The spirit of RTI directly supports what we have built.
NCRWC 2002
National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution
This government-appointed Venkatachaliah Commission explicitly recommended greater public participation in constitutional processes. We have built the educational prototype of the infrastructure that commission called for. We are not doing anything the government itself has not already validated.
Law Commission
Law Commission of India — Consultation Papers
The Law Commission has repeatedly published consultation papers inviting public opinion on constitutional matters, from the Uniform Civil Code to electoral reforms. Our platform is a permanent, structured version of what the Law Commission does temporarily through PDF consultations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are you encouraging people to change the Constitution?

No. We are encouraging people to discuss the Constitution, which is a fundamental right under Article 19(1)(a) and a fundamental duty under Article 51A(h). Discussion is not amendment. Every law school, newspaper editorial board, and parliamentary debate does the same. Our platform structures that discussion and makes it accessible to citizens who cannot afford law school.

What if someone posts seditious or anti-national content?

Our platform has three layers of protection. First, posts are publicly visible and immutable, which creates natural accountability — users know their statements are permanent and cannot be deleted. Second, we have a community reporting mechanism that flags problematic content for admin review. Third, our admin panel allows us to hide posts that violate guidelines.

We have built more accountability into our system than most social media platforms. Additionally, the Supreme Court in S.G. Vombatkere v. Union of India (2022) effectively suspended Section 124A (sedition), and the new Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita does not include sedition in its original form.

This is a government matter — why are students building this?

The Constituent Assembly Debates, which produced the Constitution, were full of ordinary citizens, lawyers, teachers, and social workers — not just government officials. The Constitution itself begins with "We the People of India." Our platform returns constitutional ownership to the people it belongs to.

We are not interfering with government. We are fulfilling the democratic promise the Constitution makes to citizens. The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution in 2002 explicitly recommended exactly this kind of public participation infrastructure.

Could NiyamAI give incorrect legal information?

NiyamAI explicitly operates as an informational assistant, not a legal advisor. Every AI system that provides legal information — from government e-courts portals to legal aid websites — carries this disclaimer. Our system prompt constrains NiyamAI to constitutional content only, preventing responses on unrelated topics. The platform's disclaimer makes clear this is an educational tool and not a substitute for qualified legal advice.

Is this a political platform?

Constitutional discourse is civic, not political. We do not support any political party, candidate, or ideology. Our platform covers all constitutional articles equally. The trending algorithm surfaces posts based on engagement, not political leaning. We are closer to a constitutional encyclopedia with a discussion forum than to any political platform.

The platform explicitly prohibits partisan content in its guidelines, and the admin panel allows removal of any post that promotes a political agenda rather than engaging with the constitutional question on its merits.

Does the platform have any legal effect on the Constitution?

None whatsoever. Votes on our platform are expressions of opinion, not legal votes. Posts are citizen statements, not petitions to any court or legislature. No action on our platform has any connection to the formal amendment process defined in Article 368. The platform is a digital public square for constitutional literacy — nothing more and nothing less.

How do you handle user privacy and data?

User identities on the platform are represented by randomly generated 8-character IDs rather than real names, protecting personal identity in public discourse. Expert and Lawmaker accounts require verified credentials managed securely. The platform stores only the minimum data necessary for its function. As an educational project, no data is shared with third parties and no commercial profiling takes place.

India's Constitution begins with "We the People" but for 75 years, the people have had no structured way to engage with it. Our platform gives citizens a read-only view plus an opinion layer — exactly like a public library that also has a suggestion board. We do not amend, we do not petition, we do not lobby. We collect and organize citizen voices. Article 19(1)(a) protects the right to express those voices. Article 51A(h) makes it a citizen's duty to pursue the spirit of inquiry and reform. The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution in 2002 explicitly recommended more public participation in constitutional processes. We have built the educational prototype of that participation infrastructure. Everything on our platform is an opinion, and expressing opinions on public matters is the oldest and most protected form of free speech in any democracy.
